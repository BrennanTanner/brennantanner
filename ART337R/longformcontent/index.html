Introduction

What is Sketch Comedy?

Sketch Comedy is a form of comedy that utilizes story telling and visual comedy in contrast to stand-up or improv comedy. It differes from comedic film in that it is normally found in short form (1-10 minutes long).

Can I make Sketch Comedy?

Anybody can write good sketch comedy. Sketch comedy is different than stand-up or improv because it is relatable. Sketch comedy tells stories, and even if the stories are in wild fantastical settings, the actions of the characters mimic reality. Anyone who can relate to the human experience can enjoy and create good sketch comedy.

What do I need to do?

In this article we will go over the steps neccessary to get you started making your own sketches

  • Take Notes EveryDay
  • Push it one step to the extreme (what if…)
  • Subvert expectations (punchline)
  • Create the outline/storyboard
  • Get your props
  • Find your personal style
  • Post your own sketch

Ideas

  • "The best ideas come as jokes. Make your thinking as funny as possible."

    David Ogilvy

Ideas come when you least expect it. You cannot expect to be able to just sit down and think of good ideas when you need. You must be open to good ideas at any time.

Three tips to better ideas

Take Notes

The first thing you need to do is to keep a notebook. Or just take notes on your phone, it doesnt matter. As long as you can have it anywhere you go and can jot down ideas before they escape your brain. I keep a small notebook in my pocket or backpack wherever I go.

Write Down Everthing

Not every idea will seem like a winner. It doesn't matter, write it down anyway. Anything that made you think for more than a second. Maybe you trip over the sidewalk, have an awkward conversation, or something just makes you luagh. There are no restrictions at this stage.

Additional Details

It is important to add details to your ideas, but don't let that limit you either. You can even add notes to an idea well after the idea was thought up. This way you can have several ideas growing and evolving before they're ready to hatch

Twist It

  • Push it one step to the extreme (what if…)

    This is where some brainstorming will come in, take some of your best notes and push them into new territory. Channel your childish humor and ask the classic “what if…” question. Don't be afraid to get wacky, not all ideas will be winners, but it will unlock your creative potential.

Example:

“self-checkout machines are annoying”

What if the machine made the process annoying on purpose because of a personal vendetta against you?

What if the self check-out machine was playing in a poker game?

What if the machine threatened to blow up if you didn't have a rewards member id?

Now you can see where the story begins to unfold. These “what if” questions begin a story, and your brain fills in the details.

Subvert Expectations

Most comedy sketches will have some sort of punch line. A punchline is a crucial detail that changes the context of the previous set up. The secret to a good punchline is to subvert expectations of the audience.

This does not mean “random = funny”, punchlines need to be relatable/understandable as much as they are unexpected. Some punchlines send the sketch into a whole new level of extreme, while others pull an extreme situation back to reality. Some punchlines are very abrupt and the suddenness alone is funny, others are incredibly subtle and the underlying message or tone is what pulls it through

Example

Your “what if” question could be the punchline, or it could simply be the setup to the punchline

Turns out the self-checkout machine doesn't have a personal vendetta against you, it has a crush on you and wants to spend more time with you.

Even thought the self-checkout machine is keeps winning it turns out that its just a regular machine.

The bomb threat was just a ploy from the store to get people to use their dumb rewards system.

Prep the Stage

  • Create the outline/storyboard

    Now that you have a premise and a punchline its time to story board. Everybody has a different method, but the concept is the same. Figure out the story beats for your sketch. Figure out the theme, mood, camera angles, even music at this stage.
    Polish your dialogue. You might write down each line word for word, or you might write down general lines on a note card and take many different takes until you find the line that feels natural.

  • Get Your Props

    The best part about sketch comedy is that it is incredibly cheap to produce.
    If you have time and resources, you could certainly go out to a location, film with realistic props and multiple actors. If you can, you certainly should
    But some of the best sketches are made by one person in their living room, with a green screen, everyday objects filling in for props and edited on their phone. You can use your imagination and the improv could very well improve the sketch.

  • Get Your Props

    After you make a few sketches, you will begin to figure out what you find funniest. This is your style, do not be afraid to explore your comedy. Find what works what doesn't, play to your strengths.
    Eventually you will find a personal brand and type of sketch that will bring viewers to your channel.
    Make what you want not what you feel you have to. Don't ever make something simply for the views if you don't enjoy the content. There is no way faster to burn yourself out. Make what you want and no matter how niche, there are people out there who will enjoy your content, it might just take longer for them to find you.

  • “If you're not surprising yourself, what are you doing?”

    Joel Haver

Conclusion

Sketches are not nearly as imposing as you thought huh? Anyone can do it.
